How they differ (and why both are needed)
Arrivals curve	Offset distribution
Indexed by	absolute clock time	time-before-departure
Units	count per bucket (with quantiles)	fractions (sum to 1)

Used for	the inflow side of occupancy	deriving departures (dwell = offset − b)
The arrivals curve says when inflow happens; the offset distribution says how long each stays → together (plus b, capacity) the assembler builds occupancy.

what is p10, p50 , p90

They're percentiles (quantiles) of the forecast — the way the model expresses uncertainty instead of committing to a single number.

Definition
For a given bucket, the forecaster doesn't say "exactly 16 people." It gives a range with probabilities:

p50 (median) — the middle estimate. 50% chance the real number is below it, 50% above. The "expected" value.
p10 — the low end. Only a 10% chance actual arrivals fall below this.
p90 — the high end. 90% chance actual arrivals fall below this (so only 10% chance of exceeding it).
So for the bucket 05:30 → p10=11, p50=16, p90=22:

"Most likely ~16 people arrive. It would be unusually quiet if fewer than 11 (10% chance), and unusually busy if more than 22 (10% chance)."

The gap between p10 and p90 is the confidence band — wide gap = uncertain, narrow gap = confident.

Why we use them (not a single number)
This is the whole reason occupancy can become a seat-probability (AD4, AD8). If the forecaster only said "16," the assembler could only ever say "the lounge will have exactly N people." By carrying p10/p50/p90 through to occupancy, the assembler can compute things like "P(occupancy < capacity) = 85%" — i.e. the probability you'll get a seat — which is exactly the engine's headline output (FR9).

  arrivals distribution for one bucket:
  count →   8   11    16    22   26
            |   |     |     |    |
  prob   →  ░  p10   p50   p90   ░
            low  ◄── likely range ──►  high
Why 10/50/90 specifically
Just a common, compact choice — enough to describe the shape (a middle + how wide the spread is) without storing the full distribution. We could carry more (p25, p75) or fewer; three is the usual default. It's an impl detail (AD8 says "a few quantiles, e.g. p10/p50/p90").

So in short: p50 = best estimate, p10 = optimistic-low, p90 = pessimistic-high, and the spread between them is what lets us produce a seat probability rather than a flat number.

in arrival spread tell me what does 0.05 represent, how doe it translate to X% of a flight's lounge-goers arrive 60–90 min before departure

0.05 is a fraction = 5%. The offset distribution splits a flight's lounge-goers into "what share of them showed up in each time-window before departure." Each row is that share; they sum to 1.0 (100%).

What 0.05 means
0–30 min → 0.05 reads: 5% of a flight's lounge-goers arrive in the last 0–30 minutes before departure. And 60–90 min → 0.27 means 27% of them arrive 60–90 minutes before departure.

So 0.27 is the "27%" — fraction × 100 = percent.

Worked example
Suppose a flight departs at d = 15:00 and the forecaster expects 40 lounge-goers for it. Apply the distribution:

offset bucket    fraction   × 40 people   → arrive during
0–30 min   →      0.05         2 people      14:30–15:00
30–60 min  →      0.15         6 people      14:00–14:30
60–90 min  →      0.27        ~11 people     13:30–14:00
90–120 min →      0.28        ~11 people     13:00–13:30
120–150 min→      0.16         6 people      12:30–13:00
150–180 min→      0.09         4 people      12:00–12:30
                  ─────       ──────────
                  1.00         40 people     (all accounted for)
So "60–90 min → 0.27" means: of the 40 people, about 11 (27%) walk into the lounge between 13:30 and 14:00 (which is d−90 to d−60).

Key points
It's a shape, not counts — the same distribution applies whether a flight has 10 or 200 lounge-goers; you multiply it by that flight's demand to get actual headcounts.
Sums to 1.0 — every lounge-goer arrives at some offset, so the fractions cover 100% of them.
This is what makes it composable: demand (how many) × offset distribution (when, relative to their flight) = each flight's arrival timing — and summed across all flights = the arrivals curve.
So 0.05 → 5% → "5 out of every 100 of that flight's lounge-goers arrive in that window."
